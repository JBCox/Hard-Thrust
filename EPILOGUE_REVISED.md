# EPILOGUE

---

## Part One — Eleanor
### March 20, 2025

They wheel me to Lily's grave on a Saturday.

Late March in Portland is uncertain. Some trees budding, others still bare. Spring trying to break through. My parents walk behind the wheelchair, their shadows stretching long in the pale afternoon light. They've aged ten years in the past two.

My mother asked me yesterday: *Was it worth it? What it cost you?*

I didn't have an answer she wanted to hear.

**LILY GRACE ASHFORD**
**1997-2020**
**Beloved daughter and sister**
**"Into the wild air"**

"We'll wait over there," Mom says, giving me privacy. Dad squeezes my shoulder once, then follows her.

I sit with the headstone. Cold granite. Five years of weather already softening the carved edges.

"It's done," I tell her. "He pled guilty yesterday. Six victims. All the cases closed. He'll never hurt anyone else."

The wind picks up. Bare branches click overhead like bones.

I should feel triumph. Relief. Victory.

I feel finished.

"I did terrible things to catch him, Lil. Things that would horrify you." I close my eyes and see Josh's hands on my skin. The weight of him in the dark. The way he looked at me like I was the only real thing in his world—and maybe I was, because his world was built on forgetting. "I slept with your killer. Fell for him, maybe. In another life where none of this was real, where he was just Josh and I was just Eleanor..."

I open my eyes. Just wind. Just stone.

"But that world doesn't exist. And I finished it. For you."

I wait for something. A sign. Lily's presence. Anything.

Nothing.

Just the cold granite under my palm. Just my breath fogging in the March air. Just the tumor in my brain, patient as winter, counting down.

"I'm scared," I whisper. "Scared there's nothing after. Or scared you're there and you saw what I became. Scared you won't forgive me."

The wind dies. Silence.

"I love you. I'm sorry. I'll see you soon."

I touch the headstone one last time. My hand is shaking—the tremors are worse this week.

"Time to go, sweetheart," Dad calls.

"Goodbye, Lily."

They wheel me back to the car. I don't look back.

That night in hospice, the nurse dims the lights. Pills that don't cure, only quiet.

Mom and Dad will go home to a house with two empty bedrooms now. In a few weeks, just one.

Josh is in county lockup somewhere, awaiting trial. Six families have answers. Closed cases.

I did that.

I destroyed myself, but I did that.

Maybe that's all anyone gets after tragedy. Not healing. Not peace. Just the knowledge that you did what you set out to do.

Finished.

That has to be enough.

I take a breath. Let it out slowly.

Close my eyes.

## Part Two — Josh
### September 2025

The pills come at seven-thirty.

I've learned the schedule by heart. The medication rotation. The therapy hours. The exact dimensions of my room—twelve by fourteen, window too small to fit through, door that locks from the outside. Small details. Safe details. The kind that don't lead anywhere dangerous.

Dr. Nguyen watches me swallow. Risperdal, Depakote, Zoloft. I don't fight it anymore.

"Good," she says, checking my mouth. Routine humiliation I've earned. "Dr. Reeves at ten."

She leaves. Lock clicks. Final.

I sit at my desk. Notebook already open.

This is the ritual that matters.

**September 15, 2025**

**The Names:**

**Sarah Martinez.** 34. Mother of two. Disappeared October 2019. Dark hair. Small scar above left eyebrow. She coached her daughter's soccer team.

**Kevin Chen.** 28. Graduate student. November 2019—survived. Uses a wheelchair now. Studies mathematics. Has a service dog named Bailey.

**Jennifer Wallace.** 41. High school English teacher. May 2020. Taught AP Literature. Her favorite poet was Mary Oliver.

**Lily Grace Ashford.** 22. Environmental science student. February 2020. Thornewood Estate. Eleanor's sister. Wanted to work for the Park Service. Called her mother every Sunday. Died in the library where I slept, where I touched her sister, where I pretended to be human.

**Robert Fischer.** 56. Retired postal worker. January 2021. Volunteer trail maintenance. Had three grandchildren. Liked to hike alone. That's how I found them, the police say. The ones who went alone.

**David Park.** 38. Software engineer. August 2022. Training for a marathon. His boyfriend's victim impact statement made the prosecutor cry.

Six people. Six lives.

I write their details every morning. Someone needs to remember them as people, not as my victims. It should be me. It has to be me.

I don't remember killing any of them.

That's not an excuse. That's the horror I live with.

---

Dr. Reeves comes at ten. She's carrying two coffees, which means this isn't our regular session.

"Josh. I read Susan Ashford's victim impact statement from your sentencing. About Eleanor."

The name lands like a stone in still water.

"She died in April," I say quietly. "Three weeks after my arrest. I found out at the sentencing. I never knew."

"You loved her."

"Yes. We were both lying to each other, and somehow it was real anyway."

"Do you still love her?"

"The part of me that's real, that's aware, that's sitting here—yes. I love her. I'll die loving her." I close the notebook. "That's my punishment. Loving someone I destroyed."

"Love isn't supposed to be punishment."

"It is when you're me."

She leaves. Lock clicks.

I open my desk drawer. Take out the letter I've rewritten forty-seven times.

*Dear Eleanor,*

*I've tried to write this a hundred times. Nothing I say matters. Nothing I could offer equals what I took from you.*

*You asked me once if I dreamed. I do now. I dream of the library. Of you. Of Lily, though I never knew her—never knew her consciously, but my body knew the room where she died, knew the estate, knew enough to come back.*

*In the dreams, I remember. When I wake, I don't.*

*I don't know which is worse.*

*You gave me something I didn't deserve: the truth about myself. Most people live and die without that clarity. I'll live and die with it. That's justice.*

*The part of me that's real loved you. Still does. That's my curse.*

*I hope you found peace. I hope Lily forgives you.*

*I never will forgive myself.*

*Josh*

I fold it carefully. Put it back in the drawer. I'll rewrite it tomorrow, and the next day, and the next. But I'll never send it.

She's already gone. Has been for months now. And I'm still writing love letters to a ghost, confessing what I can't confess to the court: that somewhere in the investigation, in the pretense, in the lies we told each other, I fell in love with her.

Really in love. The kind that lasts.

And she was falling for a serial killer while hunting a serial killer, and I was falling for my hunter while hunting her, and Lily was dead in the ground, and it was all so perfectly, horrifically wrong that there's a kind of symmetry to it.

Tragedy as architecture.

---

That evening, I take my pills. Climb into bed.

The fugue state is still there. I feel it sometimes—a flicker at the edge of awareness, like seeing movement in peripheral vision. The other me, dormant but not gone. Waiting, maybe. Patient as winter.

The medication keeps him asleep. But some nights, just before the pills pull me under, I think I feel him stir.

I don't tell Dr. Reeves about those moments.

Some cages need to stay locked from both sides.

Before I turn out the light, I say the names:

"Sarah Martinez. Kevin Chen. Jennifer Wallace. Lily Grace Ashford. Robert Fischer. David Park."

Five people who should be alive. One who barely is.

That's my life now. Pills and names and locked doors. Every morning I write what I remember about the people I killed. Every night I say their names out loud.

It should be exactly like this.

It will be exactly like this.

Forever.
